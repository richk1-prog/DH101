Final Reflection

Being human in the age of AI is really about a few key things: being aware of what you actually have control over, knowing the real-world impacts of your actions, and understanding what kind of agency we’ve actually handed over to these machines. As the book Critical Making in the Age of AI points out, we are currently at a "crossroads" where digital tools are becoming so integrated into our lives that they risk pushing us into the role of users who don't have any meaningful control or understanding of what we are using. To truly be human right now, we have to develop what the authors call “critical AI literacy,” which means understanding the nature, risks, and capacities of these tools rather than just letting them run in the background.

First off, we have to admit that we don’t have total control over AI, as obvious as that sounds. You can give an AI all the advice and examples in the world to try and get a specific output, but it’s never going to give you exactly what you expected. This is because generative AI isn't really "authoring" anything; it’s more of an “uncredited remix” of human labor. When we use these tools, we are essentially becoming "co-authors with the machine," setting up a pattern for a program to follow, but the final result is often a mix of what we wanted and the machine's own primitive logic. This lack of control is a reminder that while the AI can mimic us, it doesn't actually "know" us or what we’re looking for.

Second, it’s incredibly important to understand the costs of every bit of AI use—and I don’t just mean the subscription price. There is a massive human cost that people had to go through just so you could use these bots. The changes in how we see data on the internet came from a history where "digital labor" has been constantly undervalued. As the authors mention, the web itself is built on "free" labor. Behind every AI that feels like magic, there were people—often underpaid—who were forced to look through everything on the internet and attach tags to things individually to train the models. Even the training sets used for the biggest AI models were often scraped from the internet without any permission from the original artists or authors. Being human means acknowledging that "machine-authored" work is actually built on the backs of real people whose labor is being hidden behind a clean interface.

Then there’s the issue of agency. Right now, most AI only works when we ask it to, but we’ve already given them a level of true agency that most people don't think about. There are already bots and "active websites" like Botsin.Space where these AIs can exist and communicate with each other. When we give a program a set of rules to follow, it can generate "infinite worlds" or content that we didn't specifically design. This changes how we view "play" and "rules." It’s less about doing things one or two ways and more about seeing if we can alter the rules to see what happens. In Chapter 7, the idea of how procedural generation allows for a "human–machine task sharing" that can result in things that are absurd or humorous, which often serves as a commentary on how much we rely on technology that is actually still pretty primitive.

Even in this age, creativity is still fundamentally about the human mind. An AI can churn out work endlessly, but it’s just following a "pattern". Without a human mind to come up with ideas that are truly vivid or even absurd, creativity would eventually just dry up into a bunch of repetitive data points. AI might be able to explain something "in the style of a cat," but that’s only because it's been trained on what humans find funny. As long as human imagination persists, our creativity isn't going to change because of a bot; we are the ones who decide what is "important and culturally relevant" through the act of curation. The most control I have is when I'm writing the prompt and deciding how I want the output to look—the machine is just the suggestion, not the source.

This brings up the problem of authenticity. In an age where AI is co-constructing almost every detail of our lives, you could argue that pure authenticity doesn't exist anymore. But honestly, people have been using Photoshop and editing videos for a long time, so things haven't been "purely" authentic for a while. The big difference now is that Photoshop requires actual skill to make a fake image, whereas AI just requires the skill of "typing words correctly." Because of this, the best way to find authenticity today is to look at the physical world with your own eyes. It’s not about what you see on a phone or a laptop; it's about walking around and actually observing the world we inhabit.

Our identity as humans doesn't actually have anything to do with AI. It’s about how we interact with our environments, the people in our lives, and the media we consume. AI just happens to be a part of that media now, and it’s up to us whether we let it shape who we are. Around the end of the book suggests, we should use these tools to "think for ourselves and to tinker," rather than just accepting what they give us. Authentic identities might not be "real" in a perfect sense because we are always being molded by our surroundings, but we are still the ones doing the thinking.

Ultimately, AI can’t define what it means to be human because its entire existence is based on finding patterns in what other people have already said. Part of being human is that we all have our own thoughts and we’ll never all come to the exact same conclusion. AI is reduced to patterns because that’s how it was designed, but it doesn't reduce us to patterns unless we start taking its word as fact. As long as we view AI as a suggestion or a tool for "critical making," we keep our humanity by remaining the ones who provide the "care, dialogue, reflection, and humanity" that the machine can only ever try to mimic.
